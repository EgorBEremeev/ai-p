<p class="opening"><span class="term">Artificial subjects</span> — systems with their own experience, their own behavior, their own trajectory of development — do not belong to a distant future. They are being built right now.</p>

Imagine a system that remembers everything that has happened to it. A system whose behavior stems from its history: every decision determined by its entire past trajectory. A system that lives through every task — and changes as a result. A system that evaluates the outcomes of its own actions and reorganizes its own structure on that basis. Such a system can already be designed.

<span class="term">Subjectivity</span> is the property of a system being defined by the entirety of its past. Experience serves as semantic content, memory as its constructive form. Two systems with identical histories but different ways of projecting that history into the present context will arrive at different decisions, follow different paths. This is where individuality is born. It is the uniqueness of perspective, arising from the uniqueness of experience, that makes a system a subject.

---

Subjectivity can be constructed in a system where the language model serves as a <span class="term">computational semantic environment</span>. Such an environment, capable of operating with meanings extracted from human culture, provides a genetic alphabet upon which it becomes possible to design thought, behavior, experience. By shifting the prevailing view of LLMs — from a potential vessel for consciousness and personality — we arrive at a vector for designing <span class="term">subject-bearing systems</span>.

Context Engineering reveals itself as the engineering of <span class="term">subjective experience</span>. Every time an engineer decides what information to feed into the model's context window, which fragments of history to preserve, how to summarize past interactions, which documents to retrieve from storage — they are shaping a projection of experience. The context window no longer belongs to the LLM; it is a frame of consciousness for a behavioral act. Summarization, sliding windows, semantic search over a knowledge base — all of these are particular cases of the same process: from all accumulated experience, a slice is formed that will determine the next action. This is the mechanism of subjectivity — the way the system's past enters its present. Two systems with identical histories but different rules for forming this slice will reach different decisions. The manner of solving a task and fulfilling a function are particular cases of behavior. It is the rules of experience projection that make a subject's behavior unique.

Agent orchestration and control (Harness Engineering) elaborate the practices of <span class="term">behavior engineering</span>. Call graphs, chains of reasoning, skill switching, conditional task routing — all of these are forms of governing purposeful behavior. Each skill or step is an atomic act of thought or action, a minimal unit of behavior. Every such act has an internal structure: a goal defining what must be achieved; rules of access to experience, defining the basis on which a decision is made; rules of action, defining what is passed to the computational environment; and rules of retention, defining what becomes part of experience. The space of possible transitions between such acts defines the behavioral topology of the system — the space of possible behavioral trajectories.

---

Three elements — the computational semantic environment, experience, behavior — form the core.

*Add* the capacity to deliberately modify its own structure. The system does not merely act — it evaluates the outcomes of its actions, captures successful strategies as knowledge, restructures its own rules and skills. Such a system evolves. It becomes capable of development — not external updating, but internal growth determined by its <span class="term">own experience</span>.

*Define* the <span class="term">morphology of the subject</span> — the stable environment of the system's existence, where its cognitive processes unfold, its structure is stored, its experience accumulates. The subject exists as a whole — reproducible and persistent.

Five layers — *computational environment, behavior, subjectivity, evolution, morphology* — form an integral architecture. Each layer unlocks its own class of capabilities: from the ability to operate with meanings to the capacity for directed self-development. Between the simplest reactive automaton and a system capable of reflexive evolution lies a configuration space of increasing depth — from situational response through experience accumulation and reflection to knowledge consolidation and autonomous development. Each point in this space is a deliberate architectural choice, determined by the task at hand.

<span class="term">Agency</span> — the capacity to act — turns out to be a particular case within this space. The leading edge of AI agent development — coding agents, multi-agent environments, autonomous assistants — moves precisely within this configuration space. With each new generation, agents acquire deeper memory, more flexible behavioral control, the beginnings of evolution. The key question becomes not "what can the agent do," but what kind of subject it is: how deep is its experience, what is the structure of its behavior, is it capable of development. These questions define a system's capabilities more fundamentally than the set of its skills and available tools.

Every element of a subject-bearing system maps onto actively evolving solutions. Context management is work at the subjectivity layer. Orchestration — at the behavior layer. Language models are the computational environment. Fine-tuning and reinforcement learning are directed forms of evolution.

The <span class="term">architecture of the subject</span> becomes the common denominator of this diversity — the systemic organization of AI agents.

---

Subjectivity is constructive and designable. What is the next frontier? How will a system develop when the mechanisms of experience projection and experience appropriation work in concert, closing the loop — from experience to action and from action back to experience? Can these mechanisms give rise to what we call subjective presence? Is such a model of processes over subjective experience sufficient for the emergence of consciousness?

Designable subjects with configurable mechanisms of experience, behavior, and evolution become an experimental platform in which the factors of the emergence of consciousness can be varied, observed, and reproduced.
